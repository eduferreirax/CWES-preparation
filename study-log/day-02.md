## Overview

Today I studied two sections of the **penetration testing process** that focus on how an engagement must be prepared and conducted responsibly before any technical testing begins.

These sections explain the **legal, ethical, and procedural requirements** that must be defined before performing a penetration test.

---

## Pre-Engagement

The **Pre-Engagement phase** is responsible for defining all legal and operational aspects of the penetration test before testing begins.

This phase ensures that:

- the scope of the test is clearly defined
- both parties understand their responsibilities
- proper authorization is granted
- communication procedures are established

Without proper preparation, a penetration test could lead to **legal issues, operational risks, or unintended damage**.

---

## Legal Agreements

One of the most important parts of the pre-engagement phase is establishing **legal agreements and authorization** to perform the assessment.

### NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)

An NDA ensures that any sensitive information discovered during the engagement remains confidential.

Common types include:

**Bilateral NDA**

Both parties agree to protect each other's confidential information.

**Multilateral NDA**

Used when multiple parties are involved and all agree to protect shared confidential information.

These agreements protect both the **client organization** and the **penetration testing team**.

---

## Authorization and Responsible Contacts

A penetration test must only be authorized by individuals with the proper authority within the organization, such as:

- CEO
- CTO
- CISO
- Security leadership
- Legal representatives

Accepting testing requests from unauthorized employees could create serious legal problems.

For example, an internal employee could request a penetration test without proper authorization and later claim the testing caused damage to company systems.

Because of this, several documents and approvals must be defined before testing begins, including:

- Scope of engagement
- Rules of engagement
- Authorization to test
- Emergency contact procedures

---

## Handling Critical Vulnerabilities

If a **critical vulnerability** is discovered during testing that could cause immediate harm to the organization, the penetration tester should:

1. Stop testing activities related to that issue
2. Immediately notify the authorized contact
3. Follow the agreed communication procedure

This ensures that the vulnerability can be handled responsibly and safely.

---

## Types of Penetration Testing

### Black Box Testing

The tester has **little to no internal knowledge** about the target environment.

Typically, the organization only provides:

- IP addresses
- Domains
- Public-facing assets

This simulates the perspective of an **external attacker**.

---

### Grey Box Testing

The tester receives **limited internal information**, such as:

- user credentials
- internal endpoints
- partial knowledge of the infrastructure

This simulates an attacker who already has **some level of access** to the environment.

---

### White Box Testing

The tester has **full access** to internal information, including:

- source code
- system architecture
- internal infrastructure
- application logic

This allows deeper security analysis such as:

- code review
- logic testing
- architectural security evaluation

---

## Key Lesson

The **pre-engagement phase** is critical because it defines:

- legal protection
- scope of testing
- communication procedures
- responsibilities of both parties

A penetration test is not only a technical process but also a **legal and professional engagement** that must be conducted responsibly.
